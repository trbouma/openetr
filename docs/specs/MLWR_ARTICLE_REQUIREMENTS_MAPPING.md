# MLWR Article Requirements Mapping

This document is a working implementation traceability matrix for the UNCITRAL-UNIDROIT Model Law on Warehouse Receipts (MLWR).

It lists each MLWR article, gives a short project-oriented summary, and leaves a column for mapping OpenETR implementation evidence against the article.

This is not legal advice and does not assert that OpenETR satisfies any enacted warehouse receipt law by itself. OpenETR is a signed evidence and control layer. Legal validity, authorization, protected-holder status, priority, enforcement, and local-law recognition remain questions for the relevant MLWR enactment, institutional rules, contracts, registries, courts, and policy profiles.

Primary source: [UNCITRAL-UNIDROIT Model Law on Warehouse Receipts - English PDF](https://www.unidroit.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/2024-uncitral-unidroit-mlwr.pdf).

## Article 1 Scope Analysis

Article 1 frames the MLWR as applying to warehouse receipts. It also establishes the basic concept of a warehouse receipt as an electronic record or paper document issued and signed by a warehouse operator, acknowledging receipt of goods and promising delivery to the holder.

The current OpenETR implementation should be mapped to Article 1 as an electronic-record evidence and control layer, not as a complete warehouse-receipt law.

## Article 3 Non-Derogation Analysis

Article 3 means the MLWR rules are mandatory within their scope. Parties may use contracts, platform rules, registry rules, policy profiles, and private workflows, but those arrangements cannot vary or derogate from the Model Law provisions where the Model Law applies.

For OpenETR, Article 3 is mainly a guardrail for how the project describes recognition. OpenETR may publish signed evidence of what parties did and what policies they referenced. It should not claim that a protocol event, profile setting, or private agreement can override mandatory MLWR requirements.

| Article 3 requirement / concept | Current project evidence | OpenETR generic mapping | Assessment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| MLWR provisions are not privately waivable | The MLWR profile states that legal validity, authorization, protected-holder status, perfection, priority, liability, title, and final legal effect belong to the Recognition Layer. | OpenETR events are evidence inputs, not self-executing legal overrides. | Aligned as a documented boundary. The implementation avoids claiming that publication alone determines legal effect. |
| Contracts and policy profiles may still provide context | The MLWR profile lists contract terms, institutional policy, registry/platform rules, and attestation requirements as Recognition Layer inputs. | Policy references, attestations, external references, and participant signatures can be linked to the OpenETR event chain. | Supported as evidence architecture. A policy profile can add conditions, but it should not purport to waive mandatory MLWR rules. |
| OpenETR control events do not displace mandatory law | Transfer, encumbrance, discharge, redemption, and termination events are published as signed control-relevant assertions. | `kind 31416` control events record actions; recognition remains outside raw event publication. | Aligned. A relay-visible event proves that an action was declared, not that MLWR legal consequences necessarily follow. |
| Domain adapter should not expose waiver language | The MLWR Control Desk explains that OpenETR records signed evidence while MLWR-style law, local enactment, registry policy, contract, and courts decide legal effect. | The webapp is a domain adapter over the generic OpenETR service layer. | Current language is consistent with Article 3. Future UI should avoid controls that imply users can opt out of MLWR mandatory rules. |
| Recognition profiles must respect enacted law | Future policy profiles are listed as planned work for minimal demo, registry-backed, and secured-finance recognition modes. | Recognition profiles should evaluate the OpenETR chain under a named legal/institutional policy. | Not fully implemented. Future work should include a rule that policy profiles cannot mark mandatory MLWR requirements as waived merely because parties agreed. |

Article 3 therefore reinforces the project boundary: OpenETR can make agreements, actions, signatures, attestations, and policy references inspectable, but it does not turn private protocol choices into a waiver of MLWR requirements.

## Article 4 Interpretation Analysis

Article 4 directs interpreters to consider the Model Law's international origin and the need to promote uniformity in its application.

For OpenETR, Article 4 argues against embedding one jurisdiction's warehouse-receipt assumptions directly into the base protocol. The project should provide a portable evidence and control vocabulary that can be interpreted consistently across MLWR-style implementations, while allowing local enactments and policy profiles to supply the legally specific recognition rules.

The model avoids making one domestic warehouse receipt regime the protocol default. |
| Promote uniform application | The MLWR domain adapter translates warehouse receipt workflows into a stable generic event model instead of creating warehouse-only event semantics. | `kind 31415` origin events and `kind 31416` control events provide reusable wire-level shapes across receipt types and legal frameworks. | Aligned. Article 4 therefore supports the project's layered design: warehouse receipt terms belong in the MLWR domain adapter; common control evidence belongs in the generic OpenETR core; legal interpretation belongs in the applicable recognition profile, enactment, or institutional rule set.

## Article 5 Issuance Obligation Analysis

Article 5 concerns the warehouse operator's obligation to issue a warehouse receipt for stored goods when requested by the depositor under the storage agreement.

For OpenETR, Article 5 divides into two parts:

- what the system can currently evidence: issuance of a receipt object by a selected profile;
- what the system should not claim by itself: that the warehouse operator was legally obligated to issue, that the depositor validly requested issuance, or that the storage agreement conditions were satisfied.

## Article 6 Electronic Warehouse Receipt Analysis

Article 6 is the core electronic-control provision. It requires a reliable method for identifying an electronic warehouse receipt, keeping it capable of control throughout its lifecycle, retaining integrity, establishing exclusive control by a person, identifying that person, and transferring control.

For OpenETR, Article 6 is the closest MLWR fit to the generic control graph. The protocol does not declare legal control by itself. It produces signed event evidence that a verifier policy, MLWR recognition profile, registry, court, or relying system can evaluate.

| Article 6 requirement / concept | Current project evidence | OpenETR generic mapping | Assessment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Identify the electronic warehouse receipt | `openetr issue-etr <receipt-file>` and `/warehouse-receipts/issue` hash the receipt file and publish a signed origin event. The current wire format uses `kind 1415` with an `o` tag as the object query anchor. | The receipt is a Controlled Object identified by a stable object digest and OpenETR object id. The `#o` relay query retrieves candidate origin and control events for that object. | Strongly supported as technical identity evidence. A policy profile should still define acceptable file formats, canonicalization, retention, and document presentation rules. |
| Keep the receipt capable of being subject to control throughout its lifecycle | The query service retrieves origin events and related `kind 1416` control events by `#o`, groups them by origin, follows `e` links, and derives lifecycle state and current controller. | A control graph is built from an origin event plus later control events linked to prior event ids. Lifecycle actions such as `redeem` and `terminate` are represented as signed control events. | Supported as an event-chain model. The remaining work is not to make the base protocol more legally assertive, but to define one or more MLWR verifier profiles that state which graph patterns are reliable enough to recognize as Article 6 control in a particular legal or institutional setting.

## Article 7 General Reliability Standard Analysis

Article 7 gives the reliability standard for the methods used to satisfy Article 6. It is deliberately context-sensitive. Reliability may depend on operational rules, integrity assurance, protection against unauthorized access and use, system security, regular and independent audit, accreditation or supervision, industry standards, other relevant circumstances, and proof that the method actually fulfilled its function.

For OpenETR, Article 7 should be treated as the policy and operations layer wrapped around the signed control graph. The base protocol can make evidence portable and independently verifiable. It cannot, by itself, prove that an operator's key custody, account controls, governance, security, or legal recognition framework is sufficient.

## Article 8 Depositor Representations Analysis

Article 8 moves from technical control into legal representation and effect. It treats a depositor's request for issuance as carrying representations about authority to deposit the goods, authority to request the receipt type, and disclosure of third-party rights or claims.

It does not decide whether a depositor had authority, whether a disclosure was complete, whether a third-party claim existed, or what remedy follows if a representation is false.

Article 8 therefore confirms the usefulness of OpenETR's boundary between control evidence and recognition. ## Article 9 Storage Agreement Incorporation Analysis

For OpenETR, this is another recognition-layer issue supported by event evidence. The protocol can make a receipt, agreement, reference, schema, or attestation inspectable and cryptographically tied to a control graph. It does not decide whether a referenced term is legally incorporated, whether a transferee had adequate access to it, or whether an inconsistent term is ineffective.

## Article 13 Loss or Destruction Analysis

Article 13 concerns lost or destroyed warehouse receipts. For electronic receipts, the important analogue is failure of the Article 6 control conditions or loss of control. The article also points toward replacement procedures and safeguards against two receipts operating for the same goods at the same time.

For OpenETR, Article 13 should be handled as a replacement and recognition workflow over signed evidence. The base protocol can show that an event graph exists, that links are broken, that a control chain has been terminated, that a replacement was issued, or that competing origins exist for the same underlying object. It does not decide by itself that a receipt was legally lost, that a replacement should be issued, or that the old receipt is legally inoperative.

| Article 13 requirement / concept | Current project evidence | OpenETR generic mapping | Assessment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Loss or destruction of a paper receipt | OpenETR is strongest for electronic receipt evidence, but a paper receipt can be represented by a document digest, external reference, or attestation linked to a controlled object. | Paper loss can be evidenced by signed attestations from the holder, warehouse operator, registry, court, or other authority. | Supported only as evidence. The legal procedure for replacing a paper receipt is outside the base protocol. |
| Electronic loss as failure of Article 6 conditions or loss of control | The verifier can query origin and control events by `#o`, follow `e` links, verify signatures, derive current controller, and surface broken links, missing events, competing origins, or ambiguous chains. | Electronic loss maps to a verifier-policy conclusion that the control method no longer satisfies identity, integrity, exclusive control, controller identification, or transferability requirements. | Partially supported. | Replacement receipt process | The current system can issue a new origin event and can terminate or annotate an existing graph, but it does not yet define a first-class MLWR replacement action or replacement relationship. | A replacement could be modeled as a new origin event linked to the prior receipt object or prior event, plus `terminate`, `attest`, or future `replace` action events signed by required parties. | Not yet complete. ## Article 14 Change of Medium Analysis

Article 14 concerns changing a warehouse receipt from paper to electronic form, or from electronic to paper form. It requires the prior medium to become inoperative while preserving the rights and obligations associated with the receipt.

For OpenETR, Article 14 is close to Article 13 but not identical. Article 13 is about loss, destruction, or replacement after failure. Article 14 is a deliberate migration between media. That means the event record should not merely say that the old graph ended. It should also identify the new medium, link the old and new receipt representations, and preserve evidence that the prior medium was made inoperative under policy.

| Article 14 requirement / concept | Current project evidence | OpenETR generic mapping | Assessment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Change from paper to electronic receipt | A paper receipt can be represented by a digest, external reference, scanned document, registry id, or attestation. An electronic OpenETR receipt can then be issued as a controlled object with an origin event. | Paper-to-electronic conversion could be modeled as a signed medium-change declaration plus a new electronic origin event linked to the paper receipt reference. | Mechanism exists as evidence, but no first-class change-of-medium workflow is implemented. |
| Change from electronic to paper receipt | OpenETR can terminate or annotate an electronic graph and record attestations or references to a paper receipt issued by the warehouse operator. | Electronic-to-paper conversion could be modeled as a signed medium-change declaration, a lifecycle event marking the electronic graph inoperative, and a reference to the paper receipt identifier. | Partially supported by `terminate`, `attest`, and `ref`, but those are not expressive enough on their own to make the legal medium-change reason clear. |
| Prior medium must become inoperative | The current `terminate` action can end lifecycle state under policy, and attestations can reference prior events with `e` links. | Inoperativeness should be represented by a signed event that states the old medium is no longer operative because of a recognized medium change, not merely because the receipt was redeemed, cancelled, or otherwise ended. | A dedicated action or subtype is likely needed. `terminate` alone is too generic unless paired with a required `type=change_medium` attestation or equivalent profile rule. |
| Rights and obligations should be preserved | Query output can show the origin event, control chain, current controller, encumbrances, profile metadata, and references. Outstanding encumbrances and control state can be used as evidence for what should carry forward. | A verifier should compare the old and new representations and carry forward recognized state such as controller, claims, storage terms, and warehouse obligations according to policy. | Supported as evidence, but not currently automated. A domain profile must define what carries over and how conflicts are treated. |
| Link old and new representations | OpenETR supports event references with `e`, object lookup with `o`, external references with `ref`, and named tags for structured metadata. | A medium-change event should probably include old object id/digest, old medium, new object id/digest or paper identifier, new medium, authority/signers, and references to supporting documents or registry records. | The wire-format tools exist, but the project has not yet defined canonical tags or event shape for medium changes. |
| Keep paper receipts queryable after medium change | Even when the operative medium is paper, OpenETR can keep a signed lookup trail for the receipt identifier, paper reference, prior electronic object, and medium-change event. | A verifier should be able to query the known paper receipt reference or related electronic object and determine whether the paper record is currently active, superseded, or linked to an electronic successor. | This is an important design requirement for `change_medium`: paper should not mean invisible to the control layer. |
| Prevent both media from being treated as active | The verifier can surface multiple origins, competing graphs, lifecycle state, and policy warnings. | A medium-change verifier rule should recognize only one operative medium at a time and warn if both paper and electronic representations appear active. | Not currently implemented. This is a verifier-policy requirement for an MLWR change-of-medium profile. |

Article 14 therefore suggests that OpenETR should add an explicit `change_medium` concept. This could be implemented as a new `action=change_medium`, or as a stricter MLWR profile pattern using `attest` plus `terminate`. My recommendation is to design a dedicated medium-change event shape, because Article 14 needs to distinguish conversion from ordinary termination, loss replacement, redemption, or cancellation.

The paper side should remain discoverable. If the current operative medium is paper, OpenETR should still be able to answer whether that paper receipt is the active record, whether it was converted from an electronic record, whether it has later been converted back to electronic form, and which signed event or authority supports that state.

A design note for this profile is now in progress at [MLWR_CHANGE_OF_MEDIUM_PROFILE.md](./MLWR_CHANGE_OF_MEDIUM_PROFILE.md).

## Initial Observations

The strongest current OpenETR alignment is likely with articles focused on electronic receipt identity, control, integrity evidence, transfer of control, lifecycle evidence, surrender/presentation-style actions, encumbrance evidence, discharge evidence, and queryable state.

The project does not yet attempt to satisfy article 10 as a structured warehouse receipt schema. Today, `examples/MLWR001.pdf` and uploaded receipt documents are treated as signed/digested event data rather than parsed structured receipt fields.

The project also does not yet decide protected-holder status, priority, enforcement, operator liability, warehouse care duties, or local-law effectiveness. Those should be modelled as recognition rules, attestations, external registry references, or policy profiles layered on top of the OpenETR evidence chain.
